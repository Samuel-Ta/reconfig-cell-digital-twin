\begin{abstract}
Evaluating candidate layouts of a robotic pick-and-place cell conventionally requires a
sampling-based motion plan in a high-fidelity simulator---slow and stochastic. We present a
declarative reconfigurable-cell framework (ROS\,2, Gazebo, MoveIt\,2; UR5\,+\,RG2) whose
optimizer searches station poses and visit order against a \emph{deterministic joint-travel
surrogate} ($<\!0.0003\%$ run-to-run spread) instead of the simulator, and we validate that
surrogate honestly against real planned motion. Against a randomized feasibility planner the
surrogate shows no reliable correlation; once the measurement is aligned with what the
surrogate models---an optimizing planner (RRT\textsuperscript{*}), kinematic continuity, and a
fixed visit order---it strongly predicts converged-optimal motion time (Pearson $r=0.88$,
$n=6$). Simulated annealing against the surrogate beats equal-budget random baselines, and the
gain transfers to real motion ($-19\%$). A fair five-optimizer comparison shows no method
dominates: simulated annealing is justified by cost and reliability rather than peak quality.
The search generalizes across 50 seeds and two robot arms (UR5, UR10), the robot base pose
proves cost-neutral under the cycle-time objective, and optimized configurations execute
end-to-end in the high-fidelity twin. All validation is digital-twin- and planner-based;
physical hardware validation remains future work.
\end{abstract}

robotic cell design, motion planning, digital twin, simulated annealing, surrogate

\section{Introduction}
The throughput of a robotic pick-and-place cell is largely fixed at design time by the relative
placement of its work stations and the order in which the robot visits them. Searching this
layout space is expensive: the natural objective---real cycle time---is obtained only by
planning or executing each candidate in a simulator, and sampling-based planners such as
RRTConnect~\cite{rrtconnect} are stochastic, so each configuration must be evaluated many times.

A common remedy is a cheap surrogate objective; the risk is optimizing a proxy that does not
track the quantity of interest. This paper takes the validation of the proxy as seriously as the
optimization. We build a declarative reconfigurable cell (Section~\ref{sec:system}), formalize
the layout-optimization problem and its deterministic joint-travel surrogate
(Section~\ref{sec:system}), and ask---repeatedly and honestly---whether the surrogate predicts
real planned motion time (Section~\ref{sec:gate}), reporting two failed measurement attempts
before the successful one. We then optimize against the validated surrogate, compare five
black-box optimizers under identical conditions (Section~\ref{sec:optcmp}), extend the search to
the robot base pose (Section~\ref{sec:fullcell}), test generalization across 50 seeds and two
robot arms (Section~\ref{sec:scale}), and execute an optimized configuration end-to-end in the
high-fidelity twin with a measured time decomposition (Section~\ref{sec:twin}).

\textbf{Contributions.} (i)~A reconfigurable-cell framework whose headless IK oracle is shared
between synthesis and simulation. (ii)~A deterministic joint-travel surrogate and a formal
statement of the layout-optimization problem. (iii)~A rigorous surrogate-to-real validation:
the surrogate predicts an \emph{optimizing} planner's motion time ($r=0.88$) and fails against a
randomized planner, with a clear account of why. (iv)~An end-to-end demonstration that
surrogate-driven simulated annealing yields configurations faster in real motion and executable
in the twin, with an honest boundary of where executability breaks down. (v)~A fair
five-optimizer comparison, a base-pose cost-neutrality finding, and generalization evidence
across 50 seeds and two arms (UR5, UR10).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
\textbf{Cell layout optimization.} Metaheuristics are standard for robotic cell layout:
S\'anchez-Sosa and Chavero-Navarrete~\cite{sanchez} minimize cell area and transfer distance
with a genetic algorithm; a classical commercial tool encodes the same objective with a modified
simulated annealer~\cite{barral}; Wang et al.~\cite{wang} re-optimize reconfigurable
assembly-cell layouts inside a digital twin with multi-agent swarm learning. All evaluate
candidates by executing a simulator or twin directly; we decouple the optimizer from the
simulator through a \emph{validated} kinematic proxy.

\textbf{Surrogate-assisted optimization.} Closest to our work, Kawabe et
al.~\cite{kawabe} optimize motion planning and layout jointly using a surrogate trained on
planner outputs, and a hierarchical variant couples a genetic algorithm with surrogate-assisted
RRT~\cite{kaimujjaman}; surrogate assistance is well established in evolutionary
computation~\cite{surrogatesurvey}. Both rely on \emph{learned} surrogates; ours is a
closed-form kinematic formula needing no training data, deterministic by construction, and we
characterize when such a surrogate does and does not predict real planner output.

\textbf{Planning and twins.} Our measurements use sampling-based
planners~\cite{rrtconnect,rrtstar,prm}; the per-plan variance we document is a known property of
the family~\cite{lavalle}. Digital twins increasingly support cell design~\cite{dtsurvey}:
ROS-based twin architectures~\cite{sueldo} and twin-driven reconfiguration of collaborative
lines~\cite{kousi} target \emph{runtime} support, whereas ours is a \emph{design-time} twin
whose headless IK oracle is shared, by import, between optimizer and simulator.

\section{System and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Platform}
The platform is a digital twin built on ROS\,2~\cite{ros2} and Gazebo~\cite{gazebo} with a
Universal Robots UR5 arm and an OnRobot RG2 gripper~\cite{hardware}; planning uses
MoveIt\,2~\cite{moveit} with the OMPL suite~\cite{ompl} (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}). A cell is
described declaratively in a locked schema: robot mount pose, belt and part geometry, a list of
conveyor \emph{stations} with planar poses, and a relay \emph{task} (ordered pick/place
operations). A generator realizes a configuration into the scene and task consumed by the
simulation. Two components are shared, by import, between the headless optimizer and the live
twin: the IK reachability guard and the IK scoring oracle---so a configuration deemed feasible
by the optimizer is feasible in the twin by construction. Grasping is implemented as a welded
detachable joint plus physically closing fingers; the study therefore addresses reach and
transit motion, not contact dynamics.

\subsection{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}
Let the cell contain $N$ stations ($N{=}3$ throughout). The decision variables are the station
poses, expressed in the robot base frame in polar form over the reach annulus,
$x_i=(r_i,\theta_i)$ with $r_i\in[0.60,0.85]$\,m, plus the visit order
$\sigma$ (a permutation of the stations); the full-cell variant of
Section~\ref{sec:fullcell} adds the base pose $(x_b,y_b,\psi_b)$. Station yaw is derived
(each belt faces the robot radially), and the generator derives from each station pose a grasp
target $g_i$ on the belt. The relay visits the ordered targets
$g_{\sigma(1)},\dots,g_{\sigma(K)}$ ($K{=}4$ operations for $N{=}3$: pick--place--pick--place).

The surrogate cost chains \emph{canonical} inverse-kinematics solutions: for target $g$,
$\mathrm{IK}_c(g)$ is the collision-free IK branch closest (in joint space) to the elbow-up home
configuration $q_{\mathrm{home}}$ over a fixed 40-seed bank, which pins every target to the same
kinematic branch and makes the score deterministic (measured run-to-run spread
$<\!0.0003\%$). With $q_0=q_{\mathrm{home}}$ and $q_k=\mathrm{IK}_c\!\left(g_{\sigma(k)}\right)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surrogate}
J(x,\sigma)\;=\;\sum_{k=1}^{K}\;\bigl\lVert q_k-q_{k-1}\bigr\rVert_{\infty}
\;=\;\sum_{k=1}^{K}\;\max_{j}\bigl|q_{k,j}-q_{k-1,j}\bigr|,
\end{equation}
the summed \emph{dominant-joint} travel. Under a velocity-limit-dominated motion profile the
time of a point-to-point segment is bounded below by $\max_j |\Delta q_j|/\dot q_j^{\max}$; the
UR5's six joints share one velocity limit ($180^\circ$/s), so~(\ref{eq:surrogate}) is
proportional to that time bound and all joints are implicitly---and here, exactly---weighted
equally. Section~\ref{sec:gate} evaluates a velocity-weighted variant for the UR10, whose base
and shoulder joints are slower. The optimization problem is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:problem}
\min_{x,\sigma} \; J(x,\sigma) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad x \in \mathcal{F},
\end{equation}
where the feasible set $\mathcal{F}$ is enforced by a headless validity oracle:
(C1)~every station inside a $\pm1.2$\,m arena box around the mount; (C2)~pairwise non-overlap of
the physical conveyor footprints ($0.93\times0.51$\,m oriented boxes, separating-axis test), and
non-overlap with a $0.4$\,m square keep-out at the robot base; (C3)~an inter-station clearance
gap $\geq0.18$\,m (inflated-footprint test); (C4)~each belt facing the robot within $8^\circ$;
(C5)~every grasp target reachable by a collision-free, joint-limit-respecting IK solution
against the full scene (the target's own fixture excluded). The visit order $\sigma$ is resolved
by brute-force enumeration (a traveling-salesman solution~\cite{tsp} under the same cost), so
the surrogate and every downstream real measurement share one order.

\subsection{What ``cycle time'' means here}
\label{sec:terms}
Three distinct quantities appear in this paper and are never interchanged:
\begin{itemize}
\item[$T_1$] \textbf{Surrogate cost} $J$ of~(\ref{eq:surrogate}): dimensionless joint travel
(rad). The only quantity the optimizer minimizes.
\item[$T_2$] \textbf{Planned motion time}: the summed planned trajectory \emph{duration} of the
$K$ station-to-station transfers, from the real planner at $0.3\times$
velocity/acceleration scaling. It excludes gripper actuation, pick/place dwell, spawning,
resets, and conveyor motion (conveyors are static fixtures here).
\item[$T_3$] \textbf{Full twin relay time}: wall-clock of one complete relay in the running
twin, including planning, execution, gripper open/close dwells, weld attach/detach with
verification, part spawning/reset, and homing.
\end{itemize}
Optimization targets $T_1$; validation correlates $T_1$ with $T_2$
(Section~\ref{sec:gate}); Section~\ref{sec:twin} reports $T_3$ and decomposes it, clarifying the
relation between the $6$--$9$\,s of $T_2$ and the $\sim\!140$\,s of $T_3$.

\section{Surrogate-to-Real Validation}
\label{sec:gate}
We treat ``does $T_1$ predict $T_2$?'' as a hard gate that must pass before any optimization
result is trusted. The validation set \texttt{val\_hb} contains six valid configurations
spanning the surrogate range, sharing one robot mount.

\textbf{Two honest negatives.} Measuring $T_3$ in the twin (Attempt~1) mixed in
$\sim\!130$\,s of configuration-independent overhead and $\sim\!2\times$ run-to-run motion
variance; no clean trend emerged. A headless probe (Attempt~2) removed the overhead by summing
planned trajectory duration with the twin's own RRTConnect: the Pearson coefficient
\emph{flipped sign} between repetitions ($+0.057$ at 12 plans vs.\ $-0.550$ at 40), because
RRTConnect path duration retains $\sim\!96\%$ per-plan spread even best-of-10. A coefficient
that flips sign is measuring noise; we report it as such.

\textbf{Aligning the measurement.} Minimal joint travel is the wrong target for a randomized,
feasibility-only sampler. Three changes align the measurement with what the surrogate models:
(1)~an \emph{optimizing} planner---RRT\textsuperscript{*}~\cite{rrtstar}, asymptotically optimal
in path cost; (2)~\emph{kinematic continuity}---the relay is planned joint-to-joint, each goal
being the collision-free IK branch closest to the previous joint state, eliminating spurious
elbow flips (a transfer $A\!\rightarrow\!B$ excludes both endpoint belts from the collision
world, since leaving $A$ and approaching $B$ are required); (3)~the shared TSP visit order. With
continuity, five of six configurations become deterministic (per-plan spread
$89\%\!\rightarrow\!0\%$).

\textbf{Result.} Table~\ref{tab:gate} reports the gate. Because RRT\textsuperscript{*} is
asymptotically optimal, the \emph{minimum} planned duration over 10 plans estimates its
converged-optimal path cost; the mean is contaminated by non-converged runs (\texttt{val\_2}:
min $7.35$\,s vs.\ mean $12.6$\,s). Under the min estimator the surrogate strongly predicts
motion time---Pearson $r=0.880$, Spearman $\rho=0.943$ (median: $0.704/0.543$; mean:
$0.486/0.371$)---and Fig.~\ref{fig:gate} shows the fit. The gate passes; Attempt~2's negative
was a property of the planner, not the surrogate.

\textbf{Optimizing the surrogate reduces real motion.} Optimizing station poses and visit order
against the surrogate with simulated annealing yields five winners; pooled with the six gate
configurations ($n=11$) the correlation holds ($r=0.876$, $\rho=0.900$), and the winners average
$6.79$\,s real motion versus $8.42$\,s for random valid configurations---a $19.4\%$ reduction
matching the $17.4\%$ surrogate gain of the same run. Optimizing the cheap proxy produces a real
speedup of the same magnitude.

\textbf{Velocity-weighted variant.} Weighting each joint's travel by its velocity limit
($\max_j |\Delta q_j| / \dot q_j^{\max}$ per segment) leaves every UR5 result unchanged up to a
constant factor (equal limits). For the UR10 ($120^\circ$/s base/shoulder vs.\ $180^\circ$/s
wrists) we re-scored the scale validation set of Section~\ref{sec:scale} with both variants:
within one scoring run, weighting \emph{improves} the correlation with real motion (median
estimator: $r$ $0.87\!\rightarrow\!0.91$, $\rho$ $0.82\!\rightarrow\!0.97$; min:
$r$ $0.72\!\rightarrow\!0.85$), and the two variants correlate at $r=0.84$. The unweighted
surrogate used throughout is therefore the conservative choice, and a velocity-weighted
refinement can only strengthen the arm-transfer result.

\section{Optimization and Optimizer Comparison}
\label{sec:optcmp}

Production optimization uses simulated annealing~\cite{simanneal} over station poses and visit
order, inside a propose\,$\rightarrow$\,validate (oracle)\,$\rightarrow$\,score
(surrogate)\,$\rightarrow$\,anneal loop; on the five-seed gate run it beat an equal-budget random
baseline $5/5$ ($-17.4\%$), and Section~\ref{sec:scale} scales this to fifty seeds. All
optimizer and planner settings are collected in Table~\ref{tab:settings} (reviewer-requested;
every value is read from the released code).

Is simulated annealing the right optimizer? We compare five established black-box
optimizers---Bayesian optimization~\cite{bo}, CMA-ES~\cite{cmaes}, simulated annealing, a
genetic algorithm~\cite{ga}, and particle swarm~\cite{pso}---all minimizing the \emph{same}
deterministic surrogate over the \emph{same} polar decision space (visit order fixed by the same
TSP), behind the \emph{same} validity oracle, under an identical 140-evaluation budget and
seeds; infeasible candidates receive an identical soft penalty
(Table~\ref{tab:settings}).

We report the outcome honestly: \emph{no optimizer dominates}. The top three---Bayesian
optimization ($5.74\pm0.71$), CMA-ES ($5.80\pm0.65$), simulated annealing
($5.91\pm1.03$)---tie within seed-to-seed spread (Table~\ref{tab:optcmp}). Simulated annealing
is thus not uniquely best; its justification is \emph{cost and reliability}: tied quality at
$4.7$\,s/seed with $6/6$ feasibility, whereas Bayesian optimization needs $121$\,s/seed
(GP refits dominate) and the genetic algorithm fails one of six seeds. Critically, uniform
random placement finds \emph{zero} valid three-station layouts within budget on every seed---a
valid, well-separated triple is a roughly one-percent-density target---so the gap between
\emph{any} optimizer and no optimizer dwarfs the gaps between optimizers. A secondary check
lets simulated annealing search the visit order jointly instead of fixing it by TSP: it reaches
a marginally lower cost ($\approx\!5.05$) only by spending $\sim\!100\times$ the budget and does
not change the ranking---the surrogate \emph{is} the joint travel the TSP minimizes, so the TSP
order is already optimal per placement.

\section{Full-Cell Synthesis: Base-Pose Neutrality}
\label{sec:fullcell}
Adding the robot base pose $(x_b,y_b,\psi_b)$ to the decision space (stations sampled in the
annulus relative to the sampled base) does \emph{not} reduce cost: full-cell optimization yields
$5.61\pm0.50$ (5/6 feasible) versus $6.07\pm0.72$ fixed-base---statistically equal---even though
the optimizer moves the base substantially (mean displacement $0.42$\,m, yaw up to $1.17$\,rad);
a frozen-base sanity run reproduces the fixed-mount result within noise. This is structural, not
a search failure: the surrogate and every constraint C1--C5 are computed in the robot base
frame, so the whole cell is invariant under rigid motion---the absolute mount provides no cost
gradient, and only station geometry \emph{relative} to the robot matters. Pinning the mount for
floor footprint or accessibility would require an objective term the cycle-time surrogate
deliberately does not contain. A full-cell winner with a displaced, yawed base (0.57\,m,
0.36\,rad) executes end-to-end in the twin (guard passed, relay clean at $140.3$\,s), confirming
the base-yaw realization is geometrically correct.

\section{Generalization at Scale and Arm Transfer}
\label{sec:scale}
We test generalization across many seeds and across arms, base fixed at the arena-center mount
per the finding above. On each of fifty seeds, simulated annealing beats the equal-budget
random-valid baseline: $46/50$ for the UR5 (mean $-9.2\%$) and $45/50$ for a UR10 (mean
$-7.3\%$), with optimized costs $4.99\pm0.16$ and $4.85\pm0.26$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:gen}). The UR10
is introduced by a single robot-type parameter: the UR arms share joint names, planning group,
gripper mount, and tool frame, so the longer links flow automatically into the IK and hence the
surrogate.

Sixteen configurations per robot, spanning the surrogate range, are validated against the real
RRT\textsuperscript{*} planner with bootstrap $95\%$ confidence intervals
(Table~\ref{tab:scale}). The surrogate correlates with real motion on \emph{both} arms, both
intervals excluding zero. At scale we use the \emph{median} planned duration (noisier, since
non-converged plans contaminate it) rather than the gate's principled min estimator; we
therefore do not claim the gate's $r=0.88$ at scale---these figures are an honest,
lower-bounding generalization check under a noisier estimator and a smaller planning budget.
The UR5's moderate $r=0.694$ vs.\ its gate $r=0.880$ is explained by that estimator change, not
by a change in the underlying relationship.

\section{Twin Execution and Time Decomposition}
\label{sec:twin}
To confirm an optimized configuration is not merely fast on paper, we executed the feasible
optimized winner \texttt{val\_0} (surrogate $5.17$) in the full twin: Gazebo GUI, complete
\texttt{move\_group} stack, real RG2 gripper, welded-cube carry. Across four independent twin
sessions, $13$ of $14$ four-operation relays completed successfully
($T_3 = 167.5\pm23.6$\,s over the successes), every run gripper-clear at start with \emph{zero}
planning failures; the single failure was a dropped place verification, consistent with the
framework's separately measured $93$--$97\%$ success over $30$ trials per reference
configuration (Table~\ref{tab:twin}; Fig.~\ref{fig:overview} shows the executing twin). We
emphasize the scope of this claim: these runs demonstrate \emph{executability} of the optimized
layout in the high-fidelity twin, not a statistical characterization of $T_3$.

\textbf{Relating $T_2$ to $T_3$.} The $6$--$9$\,s planned motion times of
Section~\ref{sec:gate} and the $133$--$212$\,s twin relays measure different quantities, and the
decomposition in Table~\ref{tab:twin} makes the relation exact. $T_2$ is the planned duration of
only the $K{=}4$ station-to-station transfers. The twin relay executes $\approx\!12$
point-to-point moves (approach, descend, and retreat per operation, plus grasp-verification
retreats) in wall-clock time---$29$--$61$\,s, where the spread within one session is exactly the
RRTConnect detour variance that motivated the RRT\textsuperscript{*} alignment---plus a large,
nearly configuration-\emph{independent} overhead of $113$--$116$\,s: gripper open/close dwells,
weld attach/detach with verification, part spawning and reset, collision-checked IK solving, and
homing. Layout optimization can only compress the motion component; the constant overhead is a
property of the manipulation sequence, which is why $T_2$, not $T_3$, is the right validation
target for a \emph{layout} surrogate.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Control-oriented interpretation}
The framework admits a natural control-systems reading.\footnote{This subsection distills the
control-oriented analysis contributed by the third author.} The surrogate operates in
joint space, matching the robot control architecture, and its kinematic-continuity rule is
equivalent to a soft constraint on joint-velocity continuity at waypoints: selecting each goal
on the same kinematic branch as its predecessor avoids the high-acceleration elbow-flip
transients a randomized planner would otherwise inject---which is precisely what collapsed the
per-plan spread from $89\%$ to $0\%$. Second, the surrogate predicts the \emph{converged-optimal}
solution of an optimizing planner, not the behavior of a time-budgeted one; estimator choice
therefore matters (min $r=0.88$ vs.\ mean $0.49$), and the practical recipe is: the cheap
surrogate for search, the converged-optimal RRT\textsuperscript{*} estimate for validation, and
a time-budgeted planner with accepted variance for execution. Third, the comparison reframes the
engineering problem as constraint satisfaction with secondary cost minimization: random
placement finds no feasible layout, so \emph{finding any feasible layout} is the dominant
challenge, and a suboptimal feasible layout already captures most of the value. Finally, the
demonstrated cadence---optimize in $\sim\!5$\,s, validate in the twin in $\sim\!140$\,s,
transfer between arms by one parameter---suggests deployability for reconfiguration on product
changeovers; embedded/edge studies of real-time robotic inference~\cite{ahmed} indicate such
optimization loops are compatible with on-cell compute.

\subsection{Limitations}
\textbf{Executability boundary.} The most aggressive minimal-travel winners place stations so
close together that, while the headless RRT\textsuperscript{*} probe (belt exclusion,
continuity) solves them, the twin's RRTConnect with the full gripper collision model and welded
carry cannot plan the later cramped segments. Time \emph{prediction} and full-twin
\emph{executability} are distinct guarantees; a natural extension folds a twin-executability
constraint (larger clearance, or planning with the twin's own planner) into the search.
\textbf{Digital-twin scope.} All validation in this paper is digital-twin- and planner-based;
no physical robot experiment was performed. Transfer to a physical UR5/UR10 with an RG2 gripper
is future work, and the end-to-end twin executions of Sections~\ref{sec:fullcell}
and~\ref{sec:twin} are the closest current evidence. Grasping is a welded joint---contact
dynamics, grasp planning, and force control are out of scope. \textbf{Statistical scope.} The
curated gate uses six configurations and the twin executions of Table~\ref{tab:twin} are
fourteen runs across four sessions; the at-scale confidence intervals are wide because
RRT\textsuperscript{*} consumes its full planning budget per evaluation ($\sim$minutes per
configuration), a practical cost ceiling on validation-set size. The relay uses three stations
and one part type; scaling both is future work.

\section{Conclusion}
We optimized the layout of a reconfigurable robotic pick-and-place cell against a deterministic
joint-travel surrogate and validated the surrogate with explicit attention to honesty: two
documented negative results precede the positive one, and the boundary of the positive result is
stated. Aligned with what the surrogate models---an optimizing planner, kinematic continuity, a
fixed visit order---it predicts converged-optimal motion time ($r=0.88$); optimizing against it
transfers to real motion ($-19\%$) and to clean end-to-end twin relays. No single optimizer
dominates the comparison (simulated annealing is justified by cost and reliability), the
absolute base pose is cost-neutral so only relative station geometry matters, and the approach
generalizes across 50 seeds and two arms with the correlation transferring to the UR10
($r=0.83$). The methodology---validate the proxy as rigorously as the optimization, report where
it stops holding, and test whether it generalizes---is the central takeaway.
